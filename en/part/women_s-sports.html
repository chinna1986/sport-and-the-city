Women’s Sports

«The bicycle has done more to emancipate women than anything else in the world.» Susan B. Anthony, suffragette, 1896.

The division of men’s and women’s sports is all relative. It is above all a product of social mentality: a certain vision of women’s social role and standards of femininity. Until the end of the 19th century excess movement and competition among women was considered a potential threat to women’s «calling»: the birth of children. Even on horseback women were supposed to ride in a special manner in order to conform to norms of etiquette. Increasing the number of women in sports became a factor in the movement for gender equality and emancipation of women throughout the world. The participation of women in athletic life is also a sign of the transformation of traditional society.

Overcoming Discrimination: Women and Olympics

The Olympic Games best demonstrate the gradual process of drawing women into sports. In the first modern Olympics in 1896 women did not participate. In 1952 Olympic teams had only 10% female competitors; in 1980 representatives of the «second sex» had increased to 21%. But we should not over-emphasize this apparent success—still today the percentage of women participating in the Olympic Games has remained less than 30%. Gender inequality also strengthens the inequality in the representation of other countries and continents. Only in 1984, for example, was the first Olympic gold medal won by a representative of the Islamic world—the Moroccan Nawal El Moutawakel, and 1994 the Kenyan Tegla Loroupe became the first African to win the marathon.

“Femininity” and Sport: Double Standards, Double Expectations

Certain kinds of sports, like weightlifting, American football, boxing, and auto racing are still considered «unfeminine» because of their high injury rate. Officially, female weightlifting appeared in 2000, and female boxing, despite lengthy criticism, will become an Olympic sport at the 2012 summer Olympics in London. In today’s sports world women have the right to participate in all kinds of athletics, they are organized in teams, participate in competitions, but still, the percentage of women competing in sports remains significantly lower compared with that of men. On the other hand, there are certain kinds of sports whose popularity is due precisely to female athletes. This is above all figure skating and rhythmic gymnastics, and also, to a certain extent, tennis. Social ideas about masculinity and femininity influence the image of the female athlete created in the media. Images highlighting athletic exceptionality and willpower are united with the sexuality of the female body. Women in sports are subject to a double standard: the image of the athlete with developed muscles does not always correlate with social attitudes about femininity. Femininity in sports is often expressed in clothing, such as in tennis, or in make-up, or other «feminine» attributes—for examples, manicures.
